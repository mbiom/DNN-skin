Experience the Great Books in a Fresh and Exciting Way

In this course, you will experience great works of Greek Literature in an exciting and personally meaningful way. Once a week for thirteen weeks, a maximum of 20 students from all over the country meet with an experienced instructor in an online, real-time class in which they see, hear, and speak with each other. The instructor guides conversations that invite you to share your experiences and thoughts about a great work of literature with your peers and to grapple in a dynamic way with the meaning of the work. Introduction to Greek Literature

The literature of Ancient Greece constitutes one of the crucial foundations of Western civilization, analogous in its influence to parts of the Old Testament of the Bible. From the epic poems of Homer through the foundational drama of Aeschylus to the tragic drama of Sophocles, this work presents a powerful vision of human beings coming to terms with the meaning of human existence. Even though these works were composed over 2,400 years ago, they continue to shape our own understanding of human experience. What’s more, they are a delight to read in their richness of characterization, dramatic interest, and lyric power.

The Homeric epics, the Iliad and the Odyssey, come at the very beginning of the Greek literary tradition. Composed in the 9th century B.C. by the poet we know as Homer but whom we otherwise know little about, these extraordinary works reflect the dominant religious and cultural ideals of the Mycenaean civilization of several centuries earlier that had been destroyed by internal conflict. The Iliad and the Odyssey provide the earliest and fullest representation of Greek religion and the Greek understanding of human existence.

One of the key features of Greek literature is its representation of the relationship between human beings and the gods. The Greek gods served both to orient human beings to the divine and helped them to order an otherwise chaotic existence. They represent an extraordinary literary achievement, symbolizing man’s experience of the divine in evocative characterizations that bring the reader right to the horizon of divine presence.

An equally important concept to grapple with in reading ancient Greek literature is the concept of Fate. For the Ancient Greeks, Fate is experienced as a force that predetermines every action and guarantees the outcome of that action. While this might seem to remove free will from the equation, what we see in the great works of Ancient Greek literature is a tension between human striving and Fate. The concept of Fate also reflects the attempt of the Ancient Greeks to come to terms with the inevitability of death. As tragic as the action of the Iliad is, for example, it is somehow comforting to consider that violence, bloodshed, and tragic loss is ultimately due to a force pregnant with meaning and beyond the capacity of an individual to control or fully comprehend.

Fate as a central force in the Homeric epics gives way to the more thoughtful, philosophic engagement with the meaning of human freedom that we find in Greek drama. In this work, we see human beings accepting responsibility for the consequences of their actions even as they consider the various forces that informed them. This development signals the beginning of ethics, which is one of the primary contributions of Greek culture to Western civilization. The two play cycles that students read for this class, The Oresteia by Aeschylus and the Oedipus trilogy by Sophocles, are works that reflect in extraordinary ways this questioning of the meaning of freedom and responsibility, of fate and justice. It is in this work that we see a powerful dramatization of questions that have continued to haunt the human soul up to our own day.

The Iliad

Set in the 9th year of the Trojan War, the Iliad is the story of what happens when the Greek hero, Achilles, absents himself from the war out of anger at Agamemnon, the leader of the Greek forces. On a much grander scale, the epic poem is about a civilization in disorder as the Greek and Trojan heroes and the various gods who side with or against them increasingly succumb to such forces as jealousy, anger, pride, and revenge. At bottom, the work is a foundational effort of civilizational renewal, as the poet confronts the forces that disorder existence in the soul of an individual or civilization. At the same time, it dramatically depicts the Greek awareness of the tragic limitations of human existence, including the fact that all human beings must one day die.

Homer has been called “the poet of divine presence” for his representation of how deep the roots of the human are in the divine. This is literally the case in the representation of how many of the heroes are children or descendants of gods and in how deeply involved the gods are in the affairs of the human beings. It is also figuratively the case as the heroes recognize the immortal spirit within them that causes them to seek a lasting glory. The tragic vision of the Iliad is therefore combined with a sense of man’s inherent dignity, which is depicted as a force in men’s souls that refuses to simply acquiesce in the face of tragic loss. This depiction of man’s inherent dignity is one of the most dramatic representations of its kind in all of literature.
